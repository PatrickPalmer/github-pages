Moral Perspectives Poll

1. How should moral principles be viewed across different cultures, times, and personal beliefs?

a) They are timeless and universally applicable, unaffected by culture or personal opinion.
b) They generally remain consistent on major issues, though specifics may vary with context.
c) They are shaped by relationships, community identities, histories, and traditions.
d) They are primarily aimed at ensuring the long-term well-being of societies.

2. Which moral value or virtue is essential for someone to truly be considered moral?

a) Practical wisdom (prudent decision-making).
b) Compassion and understanding others' feelings.
c) Valuing the dignity of every individual equally.
d) Seeking and experiencing happiness.

3. How are moral values and duties determined?

a) By a universal moral law that outlines our obligations.
b) By the outcomes that benefit the largest number of people.
c) By specific virtues that guide us in what a moral person would do.
d) By the nature of our personal relationships with others.

4. A morally upright person is someone who:

a) Prioritizes care and fosters strong, positive relationships.
b) Makes decisions that lead to positive outcomes for others and society as a whole.
c) Acts consistently out of a sense of duty, never treating others merely as tools.
d) Shows consistent moral behavior and virtuous qualities.

5. What defines moral rules or principles?

a) They can be applied universally and are grounded in reason.
b) They promote caring relationships and allow for contextual considerations.
c) They are based on virtues that lead to living a fulfilling life.
d) They maximize happiness for the greatest number of people.

6. What is the correct motivation for acting morally?

a) Fostering connections and maintaining relationships.
b) Enjoying doing the right thing and developing good habits.
c) Helping society and treating others well.
d) Reason alone is sufficient to motivate moral actions.

7. If someone does the right thing reluctantly:

a) They might lack empathy and a genuine connection to others' needs.
b) They may not have developed the proper desires and habits to be virtuous.
c) They are morally superior to someone who acts correctly out of desire.
d) They are focused on societal well-being and therefore act morally.

8. How should emotion be regarded in moral decision-making?

a) It’s a key part of being moral.
b) It often distracts from clear moral reasoning.
c) It shows our social nature and care for others.
d) It’s important, but not the only thing to consider.

9. What makes an action morally right?

a) It follows moral rules and is done out of duty.
b) It shows care for someone’s needs in a given situation.
c) It finds a balance between too much and too little.
d) It brings the most good to the most people.

10. If someone has one virtue, like honesty or courage:

a) All virtues are about caring in some way.
b) They will likely follow moral rules in any situation.
c) They probably have other virtues too, like kindness and fairness.
d) What matters more is the outcome of their actions, not just the virtues.

11. Is it ever right to lie?

a) To protect or help others.
b) When it’s needed to keep another moral duty in a relationship.
c) When it’s a rare exception to maintain another value.
d) It’s never right to lie.

12. How should we understand the moral standards of the military?

a) As rules and principles based on an oath.
b) By what benefits society, considering the military’s role.
c) As virtues that fit the military’s goals, like managing violence for the state.
d) Through the military’s identity, history, and traditions.
